The Pattern of Your Life

Speaking to the students of Barratt Junior High, Dr. King emphasized that youth is the most crucial period of existence. Like an architect drawing a pattern before a building is erected, the decisions made at this age serve as the guide for the structure of one's entire life.

"What you do now and what you decide now at this age may well determine which way your life shall go."

The Barratt Speech (1967)

Principle Number One

Dignity, Worth, & Somebodiness

Dr. King's first instruction: Believe deeply in your own "Somebodiness." He addressed the societal stigma placed on color and biological features with a call for radical pride.

"I Am Black But Beautiful!"

Reject the lulls of cosmetics or hair processes meant to hide who you are. Your hair is "as good as anybody else's in the world."

✓

Don't allow anybody to make you feel that you are nobody.

✓

Always feel that your life has ultimate significance.

Principle Number Two

The Determination to Achieve Excellence

Dr. King challenged students to avoid setting out for a "good Negro job." Instead, strive to be so excellent that you enter the University of Integration by sheer mastery of your craft.

Principle Number Three

Eternal Principles & Movement

A blueprint must include a commitment to Beauty, Love, and Justice. Recognizing that life is not a "crystal stair," we must maintain forward momentum at all costs.

"If you can't fly, RUN."

"If you can't run, WALK."

"If you can't walk, CRAWL."

KEEP MOVING!
